WHAT
Part One
What is the problem
we are solving?

Most organisations are trying to solve a hiring problem without first solving a clarity problem. The brief comes first. Almost nobody gets it right.

What is the problem we are solving?

Most organisations know the ambition. Fewer have translated it into the leadership conditions required to deliver it.

As a result, assessment too often relies on instinct, reputation or past experience rather than the competencies required for the role ahead.

“The brief that feels current to those constructing it may already be behind the curve in terms of what the market actually demands.”

Six questions the brief rarely answers
01
What is the board genuinely asking this leader to deliver?
Not the aspirational version; the specific, measurable mandate for the first 12 to 18 months.
02
What is the organisation's current technology maturity?
Is it a foundation the incoming leader can build on, or a constraint they will first need to resolve?
03
How much genuine executive sponsorship exists?
At what level, with what authority, and will it still be there when the difficult decisions arrive?
04
What does the culture genuinely demand of a leader here?
What has worked in the past; what has failed; and what would the organisation not tolerate regardless of results?
05
What is the organisation's honest change readiness?
Does the mandate reflect what the organisation can actually absorb, or what the board hopes it might become?
06
What does the AI, data and cyber posture require on day one?
Not in twelve months; not eventually. What is the minimum viable leadership position this person needs to hold from the moment they arrive?

WHY
Part Two
Why
this
matters.

The stakes have never been higher. Three forces are compounding simultaneously — and the organisations that get this appointment right will widen their advantage. Those that get it wrong will spend the next two years trying to recover.

Why this matters

Organisations are facing a perfect storm of converging pressures, and the margin for leadership error is narrowing.

CIOs, CTOs and their peers are more in demand than ever. The pool of leaders who can operate at enterprise level across AI, cyber and data is small and not growing as fast as the demand. Yet at the same moment, the assessment process organisations use to identify them has not moved in twenty years.

Three forces compounding simultaneously

The role has changed more in five years than in the two decades before it.

AI
Artificial intelligence
Boards are treating AI as a strategic imperative without the literacy to evaluate credible strategies. The incoming leader must translate boardroom ambition into executable programmes on foundations that are rarely ready.
Cyber
Cyber risk
Personal regulatory accountability for cyber posture has no precedent. The CIO is now an enterprise risk officer who must be able to govern, communicate and escalate at board level; not just manage technical defences.
Data
Data as competitive advantage
The capacity to acquire, govern and commercialise data separates organisations widening their lead from those falling behind. This is now a technology leadership question, not an analytics one.
The nuance problem

The right leader for one organisation often fails in another when the context changes.

Every organisation is different. Technology maturity, board sponsorship, culture, change readiness, governance architecture; the right appointment is deeply contextual. Generic assessment models were not built for this. Neither were unstructured interviews. And instinct has never been a reliable substitute for evidence.

Culture
What the organisation will accept from this leader
Assessment coverage
The cultural contract a leader will be held to is almost never named in the brief. Yet it determines whether they can act with the authority the mandate implies.
Tech maturity
Whether the foundations support the ambition
Assessment coverage
An organisation mid-transformation needs a different leader from one that is at the beginning. The technology estate a leader inherits shapes everything they can credibly promise to deliver.
Sponsorship
How much political capital the leader can actually draw on
Assessment coverage
A leader with a strong CEO mandate operates differently from one working without it. The brief must reflect the sponsorship environment the incoming leader will find on arrival; not the one the board aspires to create.
Change readiness
Whether the organisation can absorb what the brief demands
Assessment coverage
Mandates that conflate aspiration with immediate requirement are the single most common driver of early leadership failure. The incoming leader is set up to fail before they arrive.
If organisations get this right, the opportunity is enormous: a technology leader who can actually execute the strategy creates compounding competitive advantage. If they get it wrong, the cost is not just the replacement. It is the transformation programmes that stall, the talent that follows the leader out, the board confidence that takes years to rebuild, and the twelve to twenty-four months of strategic momentum that cannot be recovered.

How we are solving this; Step 1 of 5

Before any candidate is considered, we help you build the right leadership brief: one that captures the ambition, the complexity and the capabilities required to succeed.

Agency Three conducts structured briefing conversations with each key stakeholder independently. CEO, CFO, CHRO and Board member perspectives are captured through a role-specific questionnaire designed to surface what each stakeholder genuinely needs; not what they assume the others want to hear. The results are then synthesised into a single, agreed mandate brief.

How we are solving this; Step 2 of 5

Built from 7,000+ senior technology leader interviews. EQ + IQ = XQ.

A leader can be strategically brilliant and still fail to create change. They can be emotionally intelligent and still lack the judgement to operate at enterprise level.

The best technology leaders are not defined by either dimension alone, but by the way they combine both to drive execution. That combination; what Agency Three calls XQ, is what most hiring processes completely fail to measure. After 7,000+ structured interviews with senior technology leaders, Agency Three has codified EQ and IQ into ten practical competencies that reveal a leader's true execution capability: their ability to influence the board, align the organisation, make high-quality decisions under pressure, and turn complex transformation into sustained progress.


Developed in partnership with researchers at Harvard and MIT. Validated across 500+ senior technology appointments. The same ten dimensions, consistently scored, for every candidate; regardless of who conducts the assessment.
